A Personal Statement of Belief

The Nature of God

How One Reality May Become Many Minds Without Losing Its Unity

By Michael Silberman

Where Do You End?

In a famous experiment, researchers hid a person’s real hand and placed a rubber hand where the person could see it. They stroked both hands at the same time. Before long, many participants felt the touch in the rubber hand and experienced it as part of their own body.

Nothing mystical happened. The experiment revealed something stranger and more useful: the border of the self is not simply found by the brain. It is actively built. Change the flow of information and the felt location of “me” can move.

Split-brain research deepens the problem. When communication between the brain’s hemispheres is surgically disrupted, information can divide into partly separate streams while the person still appears—and often feels—like one individual. Unity and separation are not always opposites. They can exist at the same time and at different levels.

That fact gives me a new way to approach God. I believe God is the living whole of reality, and individual minds are real perspectives formed through boundaries within that whole. Separation is not a mistake that hides unity. Separation may be the method by which unity gains location, relationship, freedom, and experience.

Individuality may not be what separates us from God. It may be one of the ways God becomes present.

This is the central proposal of this paper. From it, I can give definite answers about consciousness, divine hiddenness, suffering, prayer, responsibility, and why one reality might contain many genuinely different lives.

What I Mean When I Say “God”

The word God can cause confusion because people use it to mean completely different things. One person imagines a supreme ruler who created the universe and watches it from beyond. Another means the source of existence, the deepest reality beneath everything, the source of consciousness, the highest good, or a mystery too large for human language.

That is why two people can argue about God while barely talking about the same idea. I understand why someone might reject the image of a supernatural ruler in the sky. Rejecting that image does not require us to reject the possibility that reality itself is sacred. I also understand why someone who has felt God as a personal presence might find words such as energy or universe empty and inadequate.

I do not begin with blind belief, and I do not begin by dismissing what I cannot measure. I begin with this:

God is everything, and everything exists within God.

This is a belief about the deepest nature of reality. Philosophers call that a metaphysical belief. I test it by asking whether it makes sense, explains anything, survives serious objections, and produces a life of responsibility rather than fantasy. I will state my answers clearly. Where the answers end, I will say so instead of using mystery as a hiding place.

1. God Cannot Be Somewhere Else

When many people hear the word God, they picture a supreme person standing outside the universe and controlling it. I cannot make sense of that picture. If something exists outside God, then God is not everything. If God made reality from material that was not God, where did that material come from?

My answer is that creation never happened outside God. The universe is not a product placed beside its maker. It is a real expression occurring within divine reality.

This creates the first important paradox: God must contain the world without being limited to the world as we currently know it. If I simply say “the visible universe is all of God,” I leave no room for realities beyond it. If I separate God from the universe, I divide existence into two ultimate things. I believe everything we know exists within God while God may include forms and possibilities far beyond our universe.

Philosophers call this panentheism. It resembles Spinoza’s claim that everything that exists is “in God,” although I do not accept every part of his strict determinism. My view also has something in common with neutral monism: mind and matter may be two expressions of one deeper source rather than two unrelated substances.

I call God the living whole because whole expresses unity and living leaves room for awareness, change, relationship, and value. I am not claiming God is a giant organism. I am saying reality makes more sense to me as an unfolding life than as a dead container filled with disconnected objects.

2. The Self Has a Boundary, Not an Absolute Wall

The experiments described at the beginning do not show that the self is fake. They show that the self is an achievement. The brain continually joins sight, touch, memory, bodily position, and incoming information into one working point of view.

That distinction matters. A constructed boundary can still be real. A nation’s border is constructed, yet it changes what people can do. A melody is constructed from separate notes, yet the melody is not imaginary. In the same way, the mind’s boundary creates a real individual without proving that the individual is an isolated substance.

Split-brain findings suggest that the unity of a mind depends partly on communication. When information can no longer move normally between hemispheres, awareness can divide in some respects while remaining unified in others. The self is therefore not a tiny object hidden inside the skull. It is an organized relationship among many processes.

Biology shows a related pattern. The cells in a multicellular body retain their own activity, yet evolution has integrated them into a larger individual. Biological individuality is not always all-or-nothing; it can grow through coordination and mutual control.

Together, these findings establish something more precise than another metaphor: a whole can be real without making its parts unreal, and a boundary can create an individual without making that individual absolutely separate.

I call this relational individuality. You are a real center of memory, embodiment, choice, and responsibility. But you did not create the life supporting you, the language in which you think, or the relationships that shaped you. Your individuality exists through relationship, not in spite of it.

Separation is not necessarily the opposite of unity. It may be the structure that allows unity to become relationship.

3. I Can Encounter God Personally and Impersonally

People often ask whether God is a person or an impersonal force. I do not think I have to choose only one. If divine reality contains people, then personhood cannot be completely foreign to God. At the same time, I do not imagine God as an enlarged human personality with our exact emotions, needs, and limitations.

I can encounter God impersonally in nature, mathematics, beauty, silence, order, and the astonishing fact that anything exists. I can also encounter God personally through love, conscience, prayer, grief, forgiveness, relationship, moral responsibility, and moments when a presence feels real.

I believe God can be encountered personally and impersonally because God contains both persons and the reality from which persons arise.

These experiences do not tell me exactly how God thinks or feels. A powerful feeling can come from God, from my own mind, or from both working together. I do not need to pretend I can always tell the difference. Personal language and impersonal language each reveal something, but neither can contain the whole of God.

This also explains part of what philosophers call divine hiddenness. If God is the reality within which every object appears, God cannot appear as one more object inside that reality. I cannot step outside all existence, turn around, and view the whole from a distance.

That explains why God is not physically visible, but not why divine intention often feels silent. My honest answer is that I encounter God through reality rather than around it—through conscience, people, nature, beauty, suffering, reason, and the demand to respond. Those experiences remain open to interpretation because they reach me through a human mind.

I will not blame every sincere doubter for that ambiguity. Hiddenness is a real difficulty, not proof of moral failure. It also protects me from calling every coincidence a message and every intense desire the voice of God.

4. Consciousness Is Reality Having an Inside

Consciousness is the one part of reality I know directly from within. I can doubt a theory, a memory, or what my senses tell me. I cannot honestly doubt that experience is happening while I am experiencing it.

The brain clearly matters. Anesthesia can remove awareness. Injury can alter personality. Medication, sleep, disease, and electrical stimulation can change experience. Any responsible view must accept those facts.

But a description of brain activity and an experience are not the same description. A scientist can identify activity related to seeing red, yet the measurements do not contain the redness I experience. This is the “hard problem” of consciousness: why does physical processing have an inner feeling at all?

Physicalism says experience emerges from complex brains. Integrated information theory connects consciousness to a system’s irreducible internal cause-and-effect structure. Panpsychism proposes that rudimentary experience may be basic to reality. Cosmopsychism begins with one conscious whole and asks how individual minds arise within it. Each theory solves part of the puzzle and creates another.

My conclusion begins with what is undeniable: consciousness exists. If God includes all reality, consciousness exists within God. I then take one further step. Mind and matter may be two ways the same deeper reality appears—matter described through outward relations and experience known from within.

Consciousness is not an accidental visitor standing outside reality. It is reality having an inside.

This is my proposal, not a laboratory result. It gives consciousness a genuine place in existence instead of treating every inner life as an unexplained side effect in an otherwise mindless world.

5. Why Are Our Minds Separate?

If there is one greater consciousness, why can I not hear your thoughts? Philosophers call this the decombination problem: how could one conscious whole contain many private minds?

My answer is that a point of view is created by limited access. My mind receives information through this body, nervous system, history, and position. Yours is organized around another body and history. The boundary does not have to place us in different ultimate realities. It only has to prevent all information from being shared at once.

Split-brain findings make this answer understandable. Change the pathways through which information travels and the unity of awareness can change, even inside one brain. What this establishes is that separate perspectives can arise through the organization and limitation of information rather than through absolute separation. I believe the same principle may explain how individual minds exist within divine consciousness.

I do not imagine God as one enormous human mind speaking through every person in one voice. I imagine divine consciousness as the ability of the whole to contain every inner life without collapsing those lives into one perspective. God’s awareness would not compete with mine; it would include my experience at a depth I cannot include yours.

This preserves responsibility. If God simply controlled every action, character would mean nothing. I believe our boundaries make real choice, discovery, conflict, and response possible. We participate in God, but we are not excused by God.

6. Could God Be Lonely—or Surprised?

I do not think God is lonely in the human sense. Human loneliness comes from isolation, rejection, unmet need, and the fear that nobody truly knows us. An all-inclusive reality cannot be abandoned somewhere outside itself.

But the question points toward something important. Relationship requires real difference. Love cannot exist as a relationship if there is no freedom to respond and no perspective beyond one’s own. I therefore see the many forms of life not as a cure for God’s loneliness, but as the creation of relational value: meeting, resistance, trust, forgiveness, discovery, and love become possible only where unity contains genuine difference.

Surprise creates another paradox. If God already holds one completed script of every event, nothing genuinely happens; history only reveals what was eternally fixed. I believe God may know every possibility perfectly without every future choice already existing as a completed fact.

When possibility becomes reality through an action, something new becomes actual. God does not learn because God was ignorant. God receives the lived reality of an event that previously existed as possibility. This view, influenced by process theology, allows the future to be open, our decisions to matter, and God to be affected by the world rather than eternally untouched by it.

7. If Everything Is in God, Is Evil Also Divine?

Any belief that the world exists within God must face suffering directly. The problem is not only that pain exists. It is that so much suffering is extreme, lasts so long, falls so unfairly, and appears to accomplish nothing good.

Some suffering is caused by human choices. People torture, exploit, neglect, abuse, and kill one another. Philosophers call this moral evil. Human freedom and responsibility may help explain why some of it happens.

But human choices do not explain natural evil: childhood cancer, inherited diseases, earthquakes, famine, dementia, parasites, animals eating one another, and the suffering of creatures that existed millions of years before humans appeared.

Some pain clearly serves a purpose. It can warn us of danger, protect an injury, or teach us to avoid harm. But that does not explain prolonged torture, the suffering of infants, mass extinction, or misery that destroys a person instead of helping that person grow.

Two Different Challenges

Philosophers often divide the problem into two forms. The logical problem of evil asks whether terrible suffering can exist at all if God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and perfectly good. Are those claims logically compatible?

The evidential problem of evil makes a more modest argument. It says that even if God and suffering can logically exist together, the enormous amount of apparently pointless suffering may still count as evidence against the traditional idea of such a God.

Free will may explain why people can choose cruelty, but it does not explain earthquakes or cancer. The idea of “soul-making” says hardship can help people develop courage or character. That may be true in some cases, but suffering also crushes people and leaves damage that produces no visible growth. We cannot use the word mystery as a way to make every possible event support the same belief.

My Answer: Damage Within God Is Not the Will of God

If everything exists within God, suffering occurs within God too. I cannot place cancer, cruelty, earthquakes, and grief outside the divine whole simply because they make theology uncomfortable.

But occurring within God is not the same as expressing divine goodness. Disease occurs within a living body without becoming health. A lie exists within language without becoming truth. I understand evil as real damage within divine reality, not as something made sacred merely because God contains it.

This forces me to redefine divine power. I do not believe God is a controller who individually selects every event and could prevent every tragedy without changing the nature of the world. If that were true, the scale of apparently pointless suffering would make divine goodness almost impossible for me to defend.

I understand God’s power as participatory: the power to sustain existence, generate possibility, draw beings toward relationship, receive every consequence, and act through the responses available within reality. Stable natural laws make bodies, planning, and life possible, but those same laws allow disease and disaster. Real agency makes love possible, but it also allows cruelty.

This gives me an answer, but not a complete excuse. It still does not justify childhood cancer, prolonged agony, animal suffering, or catastrophe that teaches nothing. I reject the claim that every tragedy has a hidden lesson. Some suffering is the terrible cost of a developing, lawful world; some is caused by human choices and unjust systems; and some remains without a morally satisfying explanation.

For a person in extreme pain—or a parent grieving a child—the idea that God suffers too may not feel like enough. Companionship can matter, but companionship is not rescue. It does not bring back the dead, stop a disaster, cure a disease, or explain why the tragedy was allowed to happen.

Apotheosis does not demand that grieving people find this idea comforting. Saying that God participates in suffering cannot replace prevention, justice, explanation, medicine, rescue, or human action. It means only that suffering is not believed to occur outside divine reality or to be beneath divine concern.

If divine power works through participation, medicine, rescue, justice, protection, and compassion are not interruptions of God’s will. They are among the ways divine reality resists damage occurring within itself.

What Should We Do About Suffering?

I reject the phrase “everything happens for a reason” when it silences grief, blames a victim, or excuses preventable harm. My first response should be care, protection, relief, and resistance to whatever will create more suffering.

The question “Why did God allow this?” must be joined by “What can God do through us now?”

The problem of evil remains an open wound in theology. No explanation currently available removes its full force, and intellectual honesty requires us to admit that.

8. What Prayer Means to Me

I do not understand prayer as giving new information to God or bargaining with a ruler who might help if I ask correctly. Prayer changes the relationship between my limited point of view and the larger reality in which I live.

In prayer I can become honest about fear, grief, gratitude, guilt, and desire. I can notice motives I was hiding from myself. I can become more receptive to another person’s suffering and more willing to act. Prayer organizes attention, and attention changes what I can see and what I am prepared to do.

Could prayer affect events in ways I do not understand? I remain open, but I will not promise supernatural intervention or blame suffering people for failing to pray correctly. The dependable work of prayer is not control of the universe. It is the transformation of a participant in the universe.

9. Time, Death, and Other Possible Worlds

If time began with our universe, there was no earlier moment when God waited for creation to start. Asking what happened “before time” may be like asking what lies north of the North Pole: the grammar works, but the assumed place may not exist.

I believe time is a form of order within divine reality, not a container outside God. Our universe may be one stage in a greater process, and other kinds of sequence may exist beyond the physical time we experience. I do not know which possibility is correct.

Death makes this personal. I can say that a dead person does not become something outside reality or outside God. I cannot say with equal confidence that private consciousness continues unchanged after the brain dies. I hope individual experience is preserved or transformed within divine consciousness, but I distinguish that hope from knowledge.

What I do know is that a life has already changed the whole through every relationship and consequence it created. Death can end a form without making that life meaningless or making it never have existed.

Could There Be Other Universes or Other Gods?

Some modern scientific models allow for the possibility of multiple universes. However, we have not directly detected another inhabited universe. Strong evidence might include observable effects that another universe leaves on ours, or a well-tested theory that uniquely predicts those effects and rules out ordinary explanations.

If other universes do exist, several arrangements are possible. They might all exist inside one divine reality. Each universe might belong to its own divine whole. Or both universes and gods might be parts of an even greater order.

The meaning of the word God matters here. If God means absolutely everything that exists, then having several separate “ultimate” gods would be contradictory because none would include everything. But if a god means a consciousness or creative order large enough to contain one universe, then several such gods might possibly exist within a greater reality.

I consider other divine beings or universe-sized consciousnesses possible but unknown. I will neither announce that they exist nor insist that they cannot.

10. What This Belief Requires of Me

These ideas have roots in monism, Spinoza, panentheism, process philosophy, panpsychism, and cosmopsychism. I acknowledge those connections because I do not create originality by pretending earlier thinkers never existed.

What matters is what I do with the belief. If every person participates in divine reality, nobody becomes disposable because of poverty, illness, race, sexuality, belief, disbelief, addiction, failure, or social status. Unity cannot be used to erase consent or tell people their suffering is an illusion.

If God experiences through limited lives, attention matters. Listening to another person allows a part of reality to become known where it was previously ignored. Service is not submission to a distant ruler. It is one part of the whole responding to another.

If evil is damage within God, resisting evil is spiritual work. Feeding someone, stopping abuse, correcting a lie, building housing, treating disease, and repairing harm are not secondary to theology. They are theology becoming real.

If my belief can be corrected, humility is not weakness. It is how I prevent my private certainty from pretending to be the voice of God.

11. How I Test What I Believe

1. Does it make sense?

I look for contradictions and changing definitions. If I say God controls everything and then blame human beings for every result, I have not solved the problem. I have only moved words around it.

2. What supports it?

Observation, logic, personal experience, tradition, and intuition can all matter, but they are not interchangeable. A private experience may transform my life without becoming public proof for everyone else.

3. What could change my mind?

A belief that no fact or argument could ever affect is protected from inquiry. I want convictions strong enough to guide me and honest enough to be corrected.

4. What does it produce?

If a belief produces superiority, passivity, exploitation, cruelty, or escape from reality, spiritual language does not rescue it. I look for truthfulness, courage, compassion, responsibility, and service.

Conclusion: God Is Not an Escape From Reality

I believe God is the depth, unity, inner life, and unfolding possibility of reality itself. God is not elsewhere. Nothing exists outside the divine whole, yet the whole is greater than every form it presently contains.

I believe individuality arises through real boundaries within that whole. Those boundaries give us private experience and responsibility without making us absolutely separate. Consciousness is reality experienced from within, and our minds may be places where divine reality encounters itself through difference.

I believe God’s power is participatory rather than coercive. That does not make suffering acceptable. It places greater responsibility on us, because waiting for an outside ruler to repair the world is not enough. If we exist within God, then our response is among the ways God responds.

I do not have a complete answer to death, extreme suffering, divine hiddenness, or the exact structure of divine consciousness. I have given the answers I believe follow from the central claim and named the places where those answers reach their limit.

God is not a reason to look away from reality. God is the reason reality—and every life within it—must be faced with truth, responsibility, and care.
