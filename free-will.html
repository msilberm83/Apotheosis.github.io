An Apotheosis Position Paper

Free Will, Fate, and Divine Knowledge

Freedom Within a Reality We Did Not Choose

By Michael Silberman

Abstract

Human beings experience themselves as choosing creatures. We deliberate, resist impulses, revise plans, make promises, accept responsibility, and regret what we have done. Yet every decision arises within conditions we did not choose: our biology, childhood, culture, economic position, political environment, opportunities, injuries, relationships, and the physical laws governing existence.

Religion introduces another difficulty. If God already knows everything we will do, is the future fixed? If everything exists within God, are individual choices genuinely ours? If divine power controls every event, can human beings be morally responsible?

Apotheosis distinguishes ultimate freedom from practical agency. Ultimate freedom would require being the independent author of oneself, untouched by causes one did not choose. We do not know that human beings possess such freedom. Practical agency is the conditioned capacity to understand influences, evaluate reasons, regulate behavior, and affect what happens next.

That capacity may itself be caused and unequally distributed. It nevertheless remains real in its consequences. Because no one creates their own capacity for agency, responsibility must be joined with humility. Justice must help create the conditions under which greater agency becomes possible.

Introduction: The Question Behind Every Choice

Someone insults you. Anger arises before you consciously request it. Your body tightens. Memories appear. Part of you wants to retaliate.

You did not choose the initial reaction. It emerged from temperament, past experience, present circumstances, and biological processes. Another possibility may then appear: you recognize the anger, remember previous consequences, and consider whether to speak, remain silent, leave, forgive, or confront the person carefully.

Where, within that process, does freedom exist?

If God already knows what I will choose, could I ever choose differently?

Apotheosis approaches these questions through four levels of confidence: what is affirmed, what is reasonably concluded, what remains possible, and what remains unknown. The purpose is to identify what kind of freedom human beings can honestly claim and what responsibilities follow from it.

1. What Does “Free Will” Mean?

Arguments about free will are often confused because people use the word free to describe different things.

Freedom From Direct Coercion

A person acts freely in this sense when no one physically forces, threatens, or deliberately manipulates the person.

Freedom to Act According to Desire

A person possesses this freedom when able to act according to personal motives and values. This remains incomplete because desires may arise through addiction, trauma, manipulation, or conditions the person never selected.

Freedom to Reflect Upon Desire

Human beings can evaluate their desires. Reflection allows us to ask not only, “What do I want?” but also, “Which desire should guide me?”

Substantive Freedom

Formal permission is not practical opportunity. A person may be legally free to leave an abusive job while facing hunger, eviction, loss of healthcare, or danger to dependent children.

Ultimate Freedom

Ultimate freedom would require a person to be the true origin of a choice in a way not completely explained by prior conditions. Apotheosis does not assume this exists merely because people experience themselves as choosing.

2. The Conditions We Did Not Choose

No human being creates the starting conditions of their existence. We do not choose our genes, nervous system, family, nation, historical period, earliest experiences, initial temperament, childhood safety or trauma, many illnesses, or the opportunities available to us.

These conditions influence what we notice, fear, desire, believe, and consider possible. Recognizing their influence does not prove that choice is unreal. It establishes that freedom cannot honestly be described as unlimited independence from circumstance.

Human beings participate in writing their lives, but none of us begins with a blank page, chooses the language, or writes without influence from previous chapters.

3. Structural Freedom and Systemic Coercion

Freedom is constrained not only by biology and personal history but also by institutions. Poverty, discrimination, inadequate education, unsafe housing, political repression, medical inaccessibility, exploitative employment, unequal policing, and inherited wealth shape the choices available to a person.

Formal freedom means that an action is legally permitted. Substantive freedom means that a person possesses the actual security, information, resources, and opportunity needed to pursue it.

What appears to be personal failure may partly reflect systematically restricted possibility. What appears to be personal achievement may partly reflect opportunities the successful person did not create.

A society that values freedom must examine whether its institutions provide meaningful opportunities to understand, choose, recover, participate, and flourish.

4. Determinism

Determinism is the view that every event follows necessarily from prior conditions together with the laws of nature. If it is true, every human decision belongs to an uninterrupted causal history. A choice may arise through reasoning, but the reasoning itself arises from earlier causes.

Human deliberation is also part of the causal world. Reasons, education, laws, relationships, therapy, and reflection affect behavior. Under determinism, deliberation does not escape causation. It becomes one of the causes through which events unfold.

A person who pauses to reflect did not necessarily create that capacity. It may result from genetics, education, safety, treatment, relationships, and previous causes. Determinism permits us to say reflection changes outcomes; it does not establish that the reflective person deserves ultimate credit for becoming reflective.

5. Indeterminism Does Not Automatically Create Freedom

An undetermined event is not necessarily a free choice. If a decision were partly produced by a random quantum event, the randomness would not automatically make the decision more mine.

Freedom cannot be established merely by finding a break in determinism. Libertarian freedom requires an explanation of how a person can originate an action without that action being either determined or random. No generally accepted explanation presently exists.

6. Three Major Philosophical Positions

Libertarian Free Will

Libertarianism holds that genuine freedom requires alternatives not completely determined by prior conditions. It preserves a powerful form of authorship but faces the difficulty of explaining how an undetermined choice belongs to a person rather than to chance.

Hard Determinism and Free-Will Skepticism

These positions deny that people possess the ultimate control required for basic moral desert. This encourages humility but need not eliminate protection, rehabilitation, boundaries, education, restitution, or prevention.

Compatibilism

Compatibilism holds that meaningful freedom can exist when action arises through a person’s reasoning, values, intentions, and desires rather than direct coercion or incapacitating compulsion. It captures important distinctions but cannot quietly borrow the moral language of ultimate self-authorship.

7. The Domino Challenge

If determinism is true, the saint who cultivates awareness and the criminal who reacts blindly are both dominoes falling exactly as prior causes require.

Apotheosis accepts the force of this objection. If determinism is true, awareness, discipline, and the impulse to seek treatment or reconsider a belief are caused.

Under strict determinism, a person probably lacks ultimate authorship. Yet a person may still possess capacities that change behavior: representing alternatives, anticipating consequences, examining motives, understanding moral reasons, and learning from criticism.

Apotheosis calls this practical agency. The distinction is functional, not metaphysical. Because the capacity itself is unearned, it cannot justify spiritual superiority.

No one can take ultimate credit for possessing the conditions that made wisdom possible.

8. What Neuroscience Can—and Cannot—Tell Us

Experiments associated with Benjamin Libet found measurable brain activity before participants reported conscious intention. Later studies predicted simple decisions slightly better than chance before participants reported deciding.

These results are frequently exaggerated. The tasks involved arbitrary movements rather than complex moral decisions. Brain activity is expected because the brain is part of the person, reported timing is uncertain, prediction was not certainty, and readiness activity may represent preparation rather than a completed unconscious decision.

Human decisions begin through processes that are not entirely conscious. Conscious awareness participates in a larger biological system rather than issuing commands from outside it.

Neuroscience undermines the image of an independent conscious commander. It does not establish that reflection, self-regulation, or practical agency are unreal.

9. Freedom as a Developed but Unearned Capacity

Two people may experience anger. One reacts immediately. The other notices the feeling, recognizes its source, considers consequences, and selects a response. Neither chose the initial feeling or created the full history producing the response. Yet the second possesses a wider functional capacity.

Practical agency can be strengthened through education, therapy, meditation, recovery programs, stable housing, healthcare, supportive relationships, and accountable communities. These influences do not make a person uncaused. They become new causes capable of producing different behavior.

If practical agency depends upon conditions, a just society should help create those conditions rather than merely punish people for lacking them.

10. Divine Knowledge and a Fixed Future

The theological problem appears simple: if God already knows what a person will do and cannot be mistaken, the person seems unable to do otherwise.

Knowledge and Causation

Knowing an event is not the same as causing it. But divine foreknowledge is supposedly true before the decision, creating a deeper problem than ordinary observation.

Timeless Knowledge

Some traditions hold that every time is immediately present to God. This removes prediction but may not restore an open future if tomorrow’s action already exists timelessly as a fact.

An Open Future

God may know every possible future perfectly without treating an unsettled future as complete. Knowing possibility as possibility would not necessarily be ignorance.

Molinism

Molinism claims God knows what every possible free creature would do in every circumstance and selects which world to create. But if God chooses a complete world while infallibly knowing every decision within it, God functionally selects the history containing those decisions. Molinism may preserve voluntary action while struggling to preserve an open future.

A Participatory God

Process and participatory models propose that God knows the past and present completely, understands every possibility, and participates in an unfolding world without treating future free decisions as completed facts.

11. The Apotheosis Position on Divine Foreknowledge

Apotheosis does not claim to know whether the future is fixed, open, or beyond those human categories.

God knows reality as it truly is. If the future already exists as a settled fact, divine knowledge includes that fact. If it exists as genuine possibility, divine knowledge includes every possibility without falsely treating an unfinished event as complete.

How divine knowledge relates to time remains unknown.

12. Freedom Within the Divine Whole

If everything exists within God, it does not necessarily follow that God individually dictates every action. Apotheosis affirms relational individuality: persons are genuine centers of experience and responsibility, not disposable illusions.

Finite agency may be a localized expression of divine creativity—the capacity of reality to generate perspectives, responses, and relationships that are meaningfully distinct.

This remains possible rather than proven.

13. Does God Limit Divine Control?

Some traditions claim nothing occurs outside God’s control. Others believe God permits genuine creaturely freedom. But voluntary restraint cannot fully explain extreme suffering.

Freedom may help explain why God does not prevent every harmful human decision. It does not explain cancer, earthquakes, genetic disease, or suffering unrelated to moral choice.

Freedom may involve risk, but invoking freedom must never become a way of minimizing victims’ suffering or excusing preventable harm.

14. Prayer Without Magical Control

Apotheosis finds no reliable basis for promising that petitionary prayer routinely changes external outcomes through supernatural intervention. People pray for children who still die, wars that continue, cancers that progress, and disasters that arrive.

If God redirects one illness or accident in response to prayer but does not prevent another person’s torture or death, divine protection appears morally and evidentially uneven.

Prayer may still focus attention, expose conscience, regulate emotion, strengthen commitment, create solidarity, move people toward action, and deepen an experienced relationship with God. It must never replace medical care, protection, practical action, or responsibility.

Pray as an act of attention, relationship, and moral formation—not as a guarantee that reality will be supernaturally rearranged according to desire.

15. Moral Responsibility Without Ultimate Freedom

Responsibility need not mean that someone created themselves from nothing. Accountability may remain appropriate when an action expresses intention, the person understands likely consequences, possesses a reasonable capacity for regulation, and is not acting under overwhelming coercion.

Responsibility should exist in degrees. A deliberate act differs from an accident. A healthy adult differs from a child. A calculated offense differs from behavior during a neurological crisis. A person with safe alternatives differs from someone acting under desperate structural constraint.

Accountability should protect people, recognize victims, require restitution, rehabilitate where possible, prevent future harm, and distinguish justice from revenge.

Compassion asks what shaped the person. Responsibility asks what the person did and what must happen next. Wisdom requires both.

16. The Saint and the Criminal

If determinism is true, both arise from histories neither created. The difference between them remains consequential, but it does not justify metaphysical pride in one or hatred of the other.

The saint’s conduct may deserve trust and imitation because it improves lives. Harmful conduct may require restraint, restitution, treatment, or separation. These responses need not depend upon the belief that either person created themselves independently of every cause.

Understanding why a person caused harm does not require permitting the person to continue causing it.

17. Fate, Circumstance, and Direction

Apotheosis does not affirm that every event is planned. That claim becomes harmful when applied to abuse, disease, disaster, or the death of a child.

Not everything that happens was necessarily intended, but everything that happens becomes part of the reality from which we must respond.

This does not mean every tragedy produces growth. Some injuries remain injuries. Some losses cannot be redeemed by a theological lesson. Apotheosis does not demand transformation on a schedule.

18. The Distinctive Apotheosis Position

Apotheosis proposes conditioned, developmental, and relational agency.

  • Conditioned: Decisions arise within biological, psychological, social, economic, political, and historical conditions.
  • Developmental: Practical agency can grow through awareness, education, healing, discipline, safety, and experience.
  • Relational: Agency depends partly upon knowledge, opportunity, support, and security created with others.
  • Unearned: No one creates their original capacity for reflection or self-control.
  • Meaningful: A capacity may be caused and still affect what happens.
  • Responsible: Human actions enter the lives of other beings.
Apotheosis does not currently claim that human beings possess ultimate authorship. It affirms practical agency: the conditioned capacity to understand influences, evaluate reasons, regulate behavior, and participate deliberately in shaping what happens next.

19. A Practical Test for Agency and Responsibility

  1. What conditions shaped the action? Consider biology, trauma, culture, poverty, pressure, fear, and opportunity.
  2. What did the person understand? Could the person recognize the nature and likely consequences?
  3. What alternatives could the person perceive and practically pursue?
  4. What capacity for regulation existed?
  5. Was coercion, dependency, desperation, or unequal power present?
  6. What harm occurred?
  7. What protection, restitution, treatment, boundary, or consequence is necessary?
  8. What could increase future agency?

This framework moves the question from abstract blame toward accurate responsibility and constructive change.

Conclusion

Human beings do not possess absolute independence from cause and circumstance. We do not choose our birth, biology, earliest experiences, social position, or many forces shaping desire. Our capacity for reflection is itself influenced by conditions we did not create.

If determinism is true, awareness is determined. The saint and the criminal both arise from causal histories they did not originate. Apotheosis does not avoid this conclusion by redefining practical agency as ultimate freedom.

Instead, it makes a narrower claim. Human beings possess different capacities to understand influences, represent alternatives, anticipate consequences, regulate conduct, and respond to reasons. These capacities may be caused, but they remain consequential.

Because practical agency is unequally distributed and never entirely self-created, it should produce humility. Those with greater opportunity and self-regulation inherit an obligation to help create the conditions under which others can become safer, more aware, and more capable of deliberate action.

Whether God knows one completed future, every possible future, or reality through a form of knowledge beyond human time remains unknown.

If the future is fixed, deliberation is part of the process through which it occurs. If the future is open, deliberation helps determine which possibilities become actual.

Become more aware. Create conditions in which awareness can grow. Place space between impulse and action. Use whatever agency you possess responsibly. Protect those harmed by the choices of others. Help create a society in which meaningful freedom is not merely promised but made increasingly possible.
